Quick Overview of Voting in Flux

Flux will change politics forever by connecting Australians directly into Parliament.

Flux, as a party, is a layer for the redistribution of political power. When a Flux candidate is elected they become a gateway for voters to directly influence parliament. Unlike other representatives, Flux candidates are not autonomous, and their votes in parliament are determined wholly by Flux participants.

1. GET

The first step is distributing votes. Any seats held by Flux are divided among all participants (regular voters and other parties) fairly for each issue. You receive 1 vote for each bill before Parliament.

2. SWAP

Second, you (and other participants) are free to swap votes with each other, allowing you to accumulate votes for issues you care about. Flux will automate away all the nitty-gritty to make this as simple and seamless as possible. This is where the magic happens.

3. VOTE

The last step is the actual voting. Participants can vote yes, no, or somewhere in between. Officials elected on behalf of Flux then relay these votes proportionally to Parliament.

OR

Give your votes to someone trustworthy. In Flux, nobody will force you to vote. What you might find worthwhile, though, is to give your votes to a community leader, another political party (they can also participate in Flux), a friend, or maybe all to one cause (like a charity, or activist organisation).

Save your votes over time. Because everyone is free to swap votes, you are also free to save your votes over time. This isn't as simple as putting them in a piggy bank, but Flux will ensure there is an easy way to save up votes over months or years so you can express yourself deliberately when you think you can really make a difference.

Why do we need Flux?

Our current system doesn't work well enough; politics gets in the way of policy. Flux is an incremental upgrade to democracy designed to redistribute political power, maximise participation, remove bad policy, and empower voters.

The Australian Parliament is based on exclusion and exclusivity and does not equip us with the right tools to solve modern problems to the best of our ability; Flux guarantees the best solutions will never be excluded. In order to solve problems that have never been solved before we need to try things that have never been tried before. Flux embodies this idea, and facilitates it.

We believe the best way forward is not to focus on individual solutions but to focus on how we arrive at those solutions, and how we maintain them over time.

2

What is Flux

Flux, as a party, is a layer for the redistribution of political power. When a Flux candidate is elected they become a gateway for voters to directly influence parliament. Unlike other representatives, Flux candidates are not autonomous, and their votes in parliament are determined wholly by Flux participants.

Flux, as a system, is a way to cooperate with fellow voters and distribute power in order to increase the amount of good legislation passed, and make passing bad legislation very expensive and difficult. The focus of Flux is not to be absolutely proportional, or give all voters an equal say on everything. Rather, Flux aims to solve particular problems by enabling those who are particularly well suited to solve that problem.

Flux is designed to produce the best policy above all else.

3

How does Flux work?

Flux has many features that are built on fundamentals of economics and philosophy. By providing an open system we encourage the healthy development of policy, the suggestion of new ideas, and a culture of criticism. These are necessary for a healthy democracy.

Flux is designed to offer something to everyone. It does this by enabling specialization and so provides a platform for issue based politics, something that is not possible in the generalist Westminster system. Furthermore, it allows projects of any length to be undertaken without being interrupted by the changing political landscape. Long term policy can be worked towards by dedicated individuals even if the time-frame is well beyond a normal 3 year election cycle. Short term innovative policy, or updates to past policy can be made quickly with similar ease, without requiring prior political interest. Provided the policy is of high quality, Flux is designed to make this as simple and painless as possible.

Neutrality and openness to alternate ideas are crucial to Flux operating as a platform that everyone can use. For this reason, Flux has no platform of its own besides parliamentary reform.

Who is behind Flux?

We are a group of motivated Australians passionate about democracy, technology, solving real problems, and permissionless innovation. (See below for leadership)

We do not believe the current system of governance is adequate for today's world, and we are determined to do something about it. We feel disenfranchised and do not identify with major political parties like the ALP, LNP, or Greens. We are particularly dissuaded by the way that every major political party has lost their integrity and become self-serving, instead of representing the interests of society.

We feel that in order to raise living standards, solve global problems, provide employment, lower crime, and solve all other social ills, we must embrace the best solution and best policy, even if we disagree with it. We must have policy backed by the best explanations, not the most voices.

If you feel as we feel, please consider becoming a founding member.

Features of Flux

Flux is a system built on solid economic foundations, and offers a wide range of features that are not offered by canonical democracies.

Empower Specialists
Politicians are middle men between specialists and policy, and by cutting them out of the picture we enable specialists to create the best policy they can, unmolested by external political interests.
Built in Progress Through Trade
Trading resources is one of the main mechanics behind both the redistribution of wealth and growing prosperity around the globe. Flux introduces this mechanic to the political arena, enabling innovators and democratic entrepreneurs to solve problems with minimal hindrance.
Fully Transparent
Flux doesn't have absolute control over the voting system, which is run on a distributed public network and is able to be verified by everyone, everywhere, constantly, and consistently.
Stops Backroom Deals (the right way)
By embracing specialization Flux does away with the "one-person-for-all-issues" model, elimintating the potential and motivation for backroom deals.
Favours High Quality Legislation
Specialists naturally produce better quality legislation than generalists (like politicians), and by introducing opportunity cost and comparitive advantage for legislators we help only the best legislation rise to the surface.
Empower Voters
Flux enables passionate Australians to become fully involved in the political process, and to focus on the issues they care most about.
Encourages Criticism and Debate
The ecosystem within Flux encourages a culture of criticism around new policy before it's enacted, and demands that policy options can withstand that criticism.
Ends Corruption
Under Flux, lobbyists wouldn't need to convince politicians, they would need to convince the people. In this way special interests become directly accountable to the community they affect, and can't elbow their way through.
Facilitates Conjecture
Flux is like an arena for ideas. By ensuring the best ideas are heard we create an open culture where conjecture is shared and innovation is encouraged and supported.

FAQ

Is membership free?

Yes. Membership is free forever. We don't believe charging a membership fee is necessary, and are working on other methods of funding to ensure we can provide open access to democracy to as many people as possible.

Does Flux have a platform?

Yes and no. We do have a platform of parliamentary reform, and we deliberately do not have any other platforms. We only seek to change the way decisions are made, not to actually make the decisions ourselves. The vision of Flux is not to be the source of new policy. The vision is to help the best policy be written.

Are membership details confidential?

Yes. Membership details will be exclusively used for party business, and never sold or willfully disclosed. Currently only Nathan and Max have access, though this will expand slightly as Flux proceeds through the registration process.

Why do I need Flux; I already have a representative?

While some representatives are good, none are good enough to represent everyone in their electorate. Too often people's concerns are ignored because their representative has different views to them. Flux believes nobody should be cut off from democracy just because they disagree with their representative.

Can I still sign up if I'm a member of another political party?

Yes! The AEC is specific in their criterion for registration: "at least 500 members who are on the Commonwealth electoral roll and who are not also relied upon by another party for registration purposes."

This means you're able to support Flux as a founding member if you're a member of any other party with a parliamentarian (so Labor, Liberal, Nationals, Greens, etc are all okay). The only time it is an issue is if you are a member of a party who does not have a parliamentarian, and are used by that party to support their registration.

How many members are required for registration?

We can submit anywhere from 500 to 550 members to the AEC for registration. However, the counter (currently reading {{flux.members}}) does not reflect how many members have valid details according to the AEC. Often people forget important details like middle names, or suburbs and postcodes on their address. This means we actually need more than 550 submissions of interest. Please keep telling your friends about Flux even if our membership counter exceeds 550, as every new member increases the chance of successful registration.

Where do you provide attribution for creative common licensed works?

Here: voteflux.org/attribution

Is your voting system the same as direct democracy?

No. Although there are similarities, they are philosophically very different. The main practical differences are:

  • Swapping votes is allowed
  • Sending votes is allowed
  • Saving 'political capital' over time is allowed
Some similarities are: conservation of votes and distributing uniformly over the population.
